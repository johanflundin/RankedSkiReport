Visited March 2020 (you were three months old, in a stroller with skis bolted to the bottom) and December 2024 (you learned to ski under candy duress).

Aspen is the resort that comes up when someone wants you to know they have money. Not necessarily their money — their access to money, their proximity to money, their willingness to spend money on a sandwich. The skiing is fine. The skiing has always been fine. The skiing is in fact the fourth thing about Aspen, behind the food, the shopping, and the people, and pretty much everyone there will agree with you about that if you catch them at the right moment in their second espresso martini.

What everyone gets wrong: Aspen isn't a ski resort. It's a town that happens to have four ski mountains, each in service of a different kind of person. Snowmass is the family mountain, mostly gentle, mostly forgiving. Ajax is the in-town mountain, vertical and unforgiving, with no green runs and a gondola that opens onto a street with a Louis Vuitton store on it. Buttermilk hosts the X Games each January and is otherwise mostly forgotten. Highlands has The Bowl — a forty-five-minute bootpack to a ridge that humbles everyone who reaches it and rewards them with a run so good it briefly ruins all other skiing. You'll spend most of your trip on Snowmass and tell people you skied Ajax.

Watercolor: Aspen downtown at night, snow falling on Victorian storefronts

Aspen at night. The only quiet thing the town does, it does after everyone has gone to dinner.

On our first trip you were three months old. I bolted skis to the bottom of your stroller and pushed you from Snowmass Village up to Sam's Knob every morning while your mother and I traded shifts on the actual mountain. You slept through all of it. You also slept through your first chairlift ride, which I'll always feel slightly cheated about. On our second trip, four years later, you had developed strong opinions about cold things, resistance training, and the combined effect of helmet, goggles, mittens, and a parent shouting "pizza, pizza!" at you — which was not as motivating as we'd hoped. The solution turned out to be candy. One Sour Patch Kid for every successful turn. Two for a full pizza wedge to a stop. Three for not crying at the lift. By the end of the second day you were skiing green runs at Snowmass, fully addicted, charting your own descent in inches between candy stations. We will not be discussing the long-term lessons here.

The other moment from that trip wasn't on the mountain. We took a horse-drawn sleigh out the Castle Creek valley to the Pine Creek Cookhouse, a wood-cabin restaurant reachable in winter only by sleigh, snowmobile, or cross-country ski. The driver handed you the reins about halfway in. You drove a horse for the first time at age five, in a snowfall, in the Colorado mountains, with complete focus while the rest of us pretended not to be terrified. The horse knew the way. We did not.

Field Guide to the Local Fauna

Husband Hunter
The Husband Hunter.
Vacationing in pursuit of a different kind of dependent. From Miami or Munich, unclear. Was once on The Bachelor or knew someone who was. Will appear at Caribou Club, Casa Tua, Kemo Sabe — and apparently nowhere in daylight.
Influencer Who Doesn't Ski
The Influencer Who Doesn't Ski.
Owns the gear but has never used it. Will set up a tripod at the base of Ajax to film herself walking past the gondola in a one-piece. Has 184K followers. They will all see the snow. None of them will see her ski.
NetJets Dad
The NetJets Dad.
Flew in Saturday morning, flying out Sunday night. Has three kids in lessons he won't watch. Has a sales call at 8 AM Monday. Talks about Aspen the way other people talk about the gym.
Financial Elite
The Financial Elite.
Old money. Recognizable by what they're not wearing (logos), what they're not driving (anything with a key fob), and where they're staying (their own house). Will never say what they actually do. Will mention they've been here every Christmas since 1987. Their kids ski like locals because they essentially are.
Founder Who Just Sold
The Founder Who Just Sold.
Sold a company last year. Will tell you the multiple if you ask, and also if you don't. Wears a fleece vest with no logo (he removed it). At Cloud Nine, will ask serious questions about wine pairings. Currently "investing the proceeds," which means he's in three group chats and an email thread about a real estate fund.

Spotted, Overheard, or Otherwise Witnessed

  • At the St. Regis bar at 1 AM: an Argentine family of seven taking up two banquettes, ordering only Don Julio. They were not on vacation. They lived here three months a year.
  • A man at Kemo Sabe spending forty-five minutes choosing between two nearly identical cowboy hats. His wife sat on a bench outside, scrolling, having long since chosen hers.
  • A man at Cloud Nine, in complete earnest, spraying a $400 bottle of Veuve Clicquot at his wife and three of her friends at 1 PM. They were laughing. The waiter was not.
  • A father at the bottom of the Snowmass kids' lesson area, to his sobbing five-year-old: "Buddy. I paid for this. I paid a lot for this."

The Hit List

1
Ski here first
Conditions-dependent. On a powder day, the Headwall off the High Alpine lift at Snowmass — I've skied it in snow so deep I genuinely wanted a snorkel. On a clear day, Highland Bowl, but only after the bootpack you didn't think you'd survive. Otherwise, the ridge skier's-left off the Ajax gondola will do.
2
Lunch on mountain
The Alpin Room at Highlands or Snowmass (proper food, no nonsense), Cloud Nine at Highlands (champagne-spraying lunch, the kind of place where someone in a hat costs the catering bill), or — if you've earned this somehow — the Aspen Mountain Club at the top of Ajax. Members only. Don't ask.
3
Dinner
Pine Creek Cookhouse — only reachable by sleigh in winter, exactly the kind of evening Aspen invented. Or Bosq, if you'd rather stay in town and pay $200 a head for the privilege of being seen paying $200 a head.
4
Stay
The St. Regis or the Little Nell at the high end. If you want to actually enjoy the trip rather than perform it, rent a condo in Snowmass Village — same skiing, half the price, no one is watching.
5
Après
The J-Bar at Hotel Jerome — historic, dim, where the silver miners actually drank — or the St. Regis bar, where celebrities show off in the early evening and the Latin American crowd takes over by midnight. Both teach you something about Aspen.
6
Don't miss
Explore Booksellers on Main Street. The book you buy in the bookstore on the afternoon you take off from skiing is the book you'll actually finish.
7
Local secret
Skin uphill from Snowmass Village to Big Burn at dawn. Properly cruel start to the day, completely empty, and by the time the gondola starts loading you've already had a workout, a view, and a moment of being the only one on the mountain.
8
The trap
Spending the entire day Kemo Sabe shopping, then a four-hour lunch, then "we'll ski tomorrow." Aspen does this to people. Don't let it.
The Verdict Sofia — what I want you to remember about Aspen isn't the candy or the horse or even the strollers with skis attached. It's the walk we took one cold evening through town, snow falling so quietly it didn't make a sound. The fanciest town in skiing, finally still. Most of Aspen is performance. The quiet walk in the snow is the only part that wasn't. I hope you find more of that. I hope you keep walking.
