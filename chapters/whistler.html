Visited March 2026, when you were five and didn't yet know how to be afraid.

Whistler is the resort named when someone who doesn't ski asks where they should ski. It is, technically, the biggest in North America — a stat that means a lot to ESPN broadcasters and very little to a man trying to find his wife after three lift transfers and a glacier traverse. The actual selling point is that it can absorb anyone: a father-in-law who skis once a year, a kindergartener who has just graduated from the magic carpet, a Norwegian touring couple planning to sleep overnight in a backcountry cabin so they can ski back the next day. We overheard them. They were serious.

What everyone gets wrong: it's world-class (true) and for everyone (false). The mountain is technically two of them — Whistler and Blackcomb — connected by the Peak 2 Peak gondola, an engineering marvel whose primary use is ferrying people who got separated from their group at lunch. The snow is heavy and frequent, sometimes morally ambiguous (the local name for the wet kind is Cascade concrete). The village is what you get when 1980s developers asked themselves what a Tyrolean alpine town might look like, having never been to one. If those things annoy you, this is not your mountain. If they don't, there isn't a more complete ski experience on the continent.

Whistler Village at night, fireworks

The village. Built in the 1980s to evoke an alpine town nobody who designed it had ever visited.

The moment I'll always remember from this trip: I convinced you to ski the Blackcomb Glacier with me. Your mother declined, on grounds of sanity. We rode three lifts to the top — Excalibur, then Solar Coaster, then 7th Heaven — and traversed onto the cornice. I went first, immediately ate it, and lost a ski tumbling down the slope. From above, your mother visibly considered our marriage. You watched all of this calmly, then jumped in without hesitation, skied down to me, and shouted up the slope: "Don't worry, Mommy! If I can do it, you can do it!" A second mother nearby asked, completely seriously, if she could borrow you for confidence. You won't remember any of this. That's why I'm writing it down.

A few days later your mother flew back to California for work, leaving you with the au pair and me with — for the first time in years — a full ski day to myself. I skinned up Blackcomb. Halfway up I fell on a patch of ice, my ski released, and I self-arrested with my poles like a man who'd watched the YouTube video about it the previous evening. At the summit I met two Slovaks living in Squamish, both on skis older than you, who invited me to drop into the Spearhead Traverse with them. New terrain, no plan, two strangers I'd known for thirty minutes, an old-school commitment to the kind of skiing where the route is mostly improvised. I came back six hours later with frozen sandwich crumbs in my pocket and the strong sense I'd done something I shouldn't have been allowed to. I'll tell you about the Slovaks when you're older.

Spotted, Overheard, or Otherwise Witnessed

  • Two locals bragging about lift-line cuts at 7th Heaven; found ten minutes later sitting next to a rock with what was almost certainly a fractured tibia. The mountain has its own justice system.
  • A father at the Excalibur queue carrying his crying son's skis, his crying son's poles, his crying son's boots, and the crying son. Six minutes from the lodge.
  • A man teaching his wife to ski using only the phrase "pizza, French fry, pizza, French fry," for forty-five uninterrupted minutes.
  • A snowboarder in the Crystal Hut line, in complete earnest, complaining that the waffle was "too sweet."

The Hit List

1
Ski here first
Symphony Bowl on a powder morning. Excalibur → Solar Coaster → keep climbing. Follow the red line on the map. By the time anyone catches up, you'll have laps.
2
Lunch on mountain
Crystal Hut. Belgian waffles arriving with more whipped cream than waffle, the menu still claiming this is not a decadent dessert. Take the lie.
3
Dinner
Sidecut at the Four Seasons. Order the steak, extra fries with mayonnaise, gin on ice. The only steakhouse in town that doesn't apologize for being one.
4
Stay
Westin Resort & Spa. Dated, but ski-in/out — and walking past your luggage in ski boots is its own victory.
5
Touring
Spearhead Traverse, Blackcomb into Garibaldi. Multi-day, needs a guide — or, failing that, two old Slovaks living in Squamish on skis older than you.
6
The trap
Whistler Village Gondola at 9 AM Saturday. Use Excalibur instead — six minutes more, skips a humanitarian crisis.
—
Après
Longhorn Saloon. Cheap beer, loud music, no one cares who you are. The way après should be.
—
Local secret
The three-hour East Coast time gap. Send a few emails before lifts open, ski all day, dinner at a respectable hour. The corporate world thinks you've worked a half day. You haven't worked at all.
The Verdict There are two reasons I kept coming back, Sofia. The funny ones you've now read. The other is harder to say out loud: there are few places more quietly beautiful than the high alpine around Whistler when you've climbed a peak yourself and you're standing alone for thirty seconds before someone catches up. Most people never find that thirty seconds. The cost of admission is the willingness to keep climbing. I hope you keep climbing.
